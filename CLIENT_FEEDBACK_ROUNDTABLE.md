## Session 1: First Impressions (Landing Page Hero)

**What participants saw:** The hero section with headline "Never Forget Anything Again," subheading about SMS-based personal memory assistant, trust indicators (bank-level encryption, instant setup, works on any phone), and the CTA to text START to +1 (855) 552-1950.

### Feedback

**Maria (Busy Parent):**
> The headline grabs me immediately. "Never Forget Anything Again" — that's literally my daily struggle. But when I read "personal memory assistant via SMS," I paused. My first thought was, is this a real person texting me? The word "assistant" made me think human, not AI. I'd like that clarified right in the hero. Maybe "AI-powered" should be more prominent up front.

**Derek (ADHD):**
> I like how clean and uncluttered it is. For someone with ADHD, a wall of text would make me bounce instantly. The trust bar with the lock icon and "bank-level encryption" is reassuring — I always wonder who's reading my texts. One thing: on mobile, tapping the "Tap to Text Us — It's Free!" button is great, but the phone number +1 (855) 552-1950 looks like a business toll-free line. That gave me a slight "is this a scam?" vibe. Maybe add a note like "Remyndrs official number" near it.

**Robert (Senior / Flip Phone):**
> I can read it fine on my grandson's computer. The text is big enough. But I have a flip phone — when you say "works on any phone," do you really mean my phone? I'd want to see that called out more specifically. Like "Yes, even flip phones." I also don't know what "SMS" means. Just say "text message."

**Aisha (Student):**
> It looks professional and modern. The gradient background is nice. My concern is that the 14-day free trial mention is in smaller text below the main CTA. I almost missed it. That's a huge selling point — lead with it bigger. Also, I'm a visual person. I'd love to see a quick screenshot or animation of what a real text conversation looks like right in the hero, not buried further down.

**James (Business Owner):**
> Looks trustworthy enough. My concern is whether this is for personal use or business. I manage 12 employees and I want to know if I can set reminders about job sites, client callbacks, crew schedules. The hero copy reads very personal — "we'll remember it for you." Could there be a line like "Perfect for work and life" to signal it's not just a personal tool?

**Karen (Caregiver):**
> "Never Forget Anything Again" hit me emotionally. I'm juggling my mom's medications, her appointments, and my own work deadlines. The instant setup claim is important to me — I don't have time to learn a new system. But I'd want to see "How fast is instant?" Maybe "Set up in 60 seconds" is more concrete.

## Session 3: Features & How It Works

**What participants saw:** The "Why Remyndrs" 6-feature grid (No App Required, Natural Language, Reminders That Work, Private & Secure, Works on Any Phone, Personal Memory Bank) and the 3-step "How It Works" section.

### Feedback

**Maria (Busy Parent):**
> The 3-step "How It Works" is crystal clear: text it, we process it, get reminded. Love the simplicity. But "We Process It" as step 2 feels vague and slightly ominous. Process how? Where does it go? A quick line like "Our AI reads your message and sets everything up" would be more reassuring.

**Derek (ADHD):**
> "Natural Language" is the feature that matters most to me. No special commands, no formatting — just text like you're texting a friend. The feature description says this, but I'd love to see a side-by-side: "Other apps: /remind 2024-02-21 14:00 Call dentist" vs. "Remyndrs: Remind me to call the dentist tomorrow at 2." That contrast would sell it instantly.

**Robert (Senior / Flip Phone):**
> "Works on Any Phone" — this is the feature I care about. The description mentions flip phones and that's great. But I have a specific concern: my flip phone has a character limit on texts. What happens if my text is too long? Does it still work? I'd want that addressed in the FAQ. Also, "Natural Language Processing" means nothing to me. Say "Just text normally."

**Aisha (Student):**
> "Personal Memory Bank" is underrated. The ability to save and recall information — WiFi passwords, clothing sizes, random facts — is something Notion and Apple Notes do, but they require me to open an app and organize things. The fact that I can just text "remember my locker combo is 24-8-16" and retrieve it later is genuinely novel. I'd make this feature more prominent.

**James (Business Owner):**
> "Private & Secure" needs more substance. "Bank-level encryption" is a buzzword everyone uses. For a business owner sending potentially sensitive client info via text ("remember Johnson Electric owes us $4,200"), I need to know specifically: Is data encrypted at rest? Who has access? Is there a data retention policy? The privacy page covers some of this, but a brief "Your data is encrypted with AES-256 at rest and TLS in transit" right on the feature card would go a long way.

**Karen (Caregiver):**
> I want to know about shared access. Can I set up Remyndrs for my mom from my phone? Can we both receive the same reminders? The features listed are all individual-user focused. A caregiver managing someone else's reminders is a huge use case that isn't addressed anywhere on the site. Even a FAQ entry would help.

The problem is the Free tier is almost unusable — 2 reminders per day? I set 5-6 reminders before breakfast. It feels like the free tier exists just to frustrate people into upgrading rather than providing genuine value. 3-5 reminders/day on the free tier would feel more generous and less manipulative.

**Derek (ADHD):**

The site says I get reminders at 7 days, 1 day, and on the last day — smart. For the pricing itself, $8.99/month is comparable to other subscription services I use. It's less than Spotify. For something that genuinely helps me function day-to-day, it's worth it. But I'd want to see a "Compare to alternatives" section. Show me that a personal assistant costs $500/month.

**Robert (Senior / Flip Phone):**
> I'm on a fixed income. $8.99/month adds up. The free tier with 2 reminders might actually work for me — I mainly need medication reminders twice a day. But 5 saved memories is tight. I'd want 10-15. My WiFi password, my pharmacy number, my doctor's number, my daughter's address, my medications list — that's already 5. Could there be a "Senior" tier? **Aisha (Student):**

**James (Business Owner):**

The current Premium plan feels consumer-grade. If you want business users paying, give them business features.

**Karen (Caregiver):**

## Session 8: Follow-Up — Should "SMS" Be Changed to "Text Message" Sitewide?

**Context presented to participants:** A code audit revealed that the term "SMS" appears approximately 30 times across the site. Only 4-5 of those are in legally required carrier disclaimers ("Standard SMS rates apply," "you agree to receive automated SMS messages"). The remaining ~25 instances are in actual user-facing copy: the hero subheading ("Your personal memory assistant via SMS"), every page title, all meta descriptions and social sharing tags, logo alt text, FAQ questions and answers, feature descriptions, structured data for search engines, and the SMS Service Terms heading. This is not a cosmetic tweak — it's a core part of how the brand describes itself.

**The question:** Should Remyndrs replace "SMS" with "text message" (or "text") throughout the site's user-facing copy?

---

**Robert (Senior / Flip Phone) — Original raiser, reaffirms YES:**
> I'm glad you looked into this. Thirty times! That proves my point. I know what a text message is. I send them every day. But "SMS" sounds like a technical spec sheet. My friends at the community center would see "personal memory assistant via SMS" and think it's some kind of IT product. "Via text message" or even just "via text" is warmer and more human. You're selling simplicity — the language should be simple too.

**Maria (Busy Parent) — YES, with a nuance:**
> Honestly? I know what SMS means, but I never use that word in real life. I say "text me" or "send me a text." Nobody says "send me an SMS." So yes, the copy should match how people actually talk. Especially the hero line — "Your personal memory assistant via text" flows better and feels less clinical. That said, in the legal disclaimers you're probably required to say "SMS" for compliance. Keep it there, lose it everywhere else.

**Derek (ADHD) — YES, enthusiastically:**
> This is a branding consistency issue. The whole selling point of Remyndrs is "just text naturally." But then the site itself doesn't talk naturally — it uses a technical acronym. There's a disconnect. You're telling me to text like I'm talking to a friend, but the website talks like a telecom whitepaper. Change the hero to "Your personal memory assistant — just text us." Change the page titles to "Text Message Memory Assistant" or even just drop the qualifier entirely. Everyone knows what texting is. You don't need to label the delivery mechanism in every headline.

**Aisha (Student) — YES, but for a different reason:**
> I actually know what SMS means, and most people my age do too. But here's the thing — "SMS" feels dated. It feels like 2010. My generation says "text" or "iMessage" or "message." Even Google calls it "Messages" now, not "SMS." Using "SMS" 30 times makes the brand feel older and more technical than it needs to. For SEO, "text message reminders" is probably a better search term than "SMS reminders" too — more people search with natural language now. Swap it.

**James (Business Owner) — MOSTLY YES, with a caveat:**
> In a business context, "SMS" is actually understood and even preferred — it sounds more professional. "SMS marketing," "SMS notifications," "SMS service" — these are standard industry terms. If you're building out a business tier, keeping "SMS" in business-facing copy isn't a problem. But for the main consumer-facing landing page? Yeah, "text message" is friendlier. My suggestion: change the consumer copy to "text message," but if you ever create a business-oriented page or enterprise pitch deck, keep "SMS" there. It signals you understand the B2B space.

**Karen (Caregiver) — YES, firmly:**
> When I was researching care tools for my mother, I specifically searched for "text message reminders for seniors," not "SMS reminders." If your site says "SMS" everywhere, it might not even show up for people searching the way I do. More importantly, when I explain this to my mom, I'd say "it's a text message service that reminds you of things." If the site says "SMS," she'd ask me what that stands for, and then I'd have to explain it, and now there's friction before she even tries it. The whole point is zero friction. The language should reflect that.
